PSD3 and PSR changes: everything banks need to know PSD3 is coming. In June 2023, the European Commission outlined its proposed updates to PSD2, actually ushering in the PSD3 era. At the same time, the UK payment system regulator (PSR) has also introduced new details for its proposal, providing more comprehensive protection for customers who have been victims of fraud. Reviewing PSD2 and SCA Before delving into PSD3 and PSR, let's quickly review PSD2. PSD2 aims to promote greater competition between banks and non-bank organizations by balancing the two. Strong Customer Authentication (SCA) is a key component of the entire regulatory framework, aimed at reducing fraud. A retrospective review by the European Commission concluded that SCA has had a positive overall impact on reducing unauthorized fraud. However, an unintended consequence of SCA is that it allows other types of fraud to appear, such as fraud. In the European market, fraud is typically referred to as impersonation. Impersonation refers to scammers contacting victims and persuading them to authorize transactions on their behalf. This presents new challenges for banks in protecting customers and better detecting fraudulent transactions. PSD3 seeks to address some of these challenges. Five key principles of PSD3 The PSD3 includes five key principles: IBAN-Name Verification These principles' first is IBAN-Name Verification. For those familiar with the UK paymentor verification model, this proposal is very similar. It allows customers to verify the person they are paying by sending communication between the bank and the receiving bank. If there is a mismatch, the customer can be warned and may avoid fraud when the fraud occurs. Enhanced data sharing The second principle is a legal basis and framework that allows banks to share data more efficiently. In many parts of the world, banks have recognized the scale of fraud and impersonation. They recognize that fraudsters exchange and share best practices. Now people realize that if banks want to stay ahead of fraudsters, they need to follow these principles. The ability to share known bad data, such as known money transfer accounts or high-risk beneficiaries, will ultimately enable all sizes of financial services organizations to make better risk decisions when transactions occur. Enhanced fraud monitoring The third principle is that banks are required to improve their performance from the perspective of fraud transaction monitoring. It is unclear what this requirement means until more details are published. However, a possible result is that banks will be forced to move from rule-based fraud detection methods to more reliance on automated machine learning detection methods. Customer and employee education The fourth principle involves the education of consumers and employee members. It is widely believed that customer education is a key pillar of any fraud and impersonation prevention strategy. Banks will have to do more in this area. We see banks moving away from traditional education awareness, with customers having to find best practices and guidance themselves. We see banks incorporating these pieces of information explicitly into the user journey during transactions. This education, if done correctly, can enable banks to make customers the first line of defense against fraud and may prevent customers from making transactions even before they reach the bank's fraud system. Employee training is also a key part of PSD3. Banks must ensure that their employees understand the latest fraud trends and remain credible and knowledgeable when customers seek advice. Enhanced customer refund rights The fifth, perhaps most important principle is that if a customer becomes a victim of impersonation fraud, they will have stronger refund rights. Currently, there is no law in the EU that requires customers who become victims of impersonation fraud to be compensated. PSD3 aims to provide some enhanced consumer protection for certain types of impersonation. It is important to note that regulations specify that only when a victim is a victim of impersonation fraud are they entitled to compensation. This leaves questions about investment fraud, romance fraud, and other types of fraud that are widely present in the market. The second regulation is that if the IBAN-Name Verification service fails, they may be entitled to a refund. However, we will wait for further details to understand the exact meaning of errors in this process. Banks face increased risk exposure This is important for banks because their risk exposure will significantly increase. Currently, they do not provide these refunds. In the future, they will have to take on this responsibility. This means they will have to find fraud budgets that they have not had before. Therefore, we expect them to double their efforts in detection and technology to offset some of the risks associated with this new set of regulations. PSR-enhanced fraud protection Returning to PSR, there are obvious similarities to what I described in PSD3. The concept of PSR is that if someone becomes a victim of fraud, it provides enhanced consumer protection. In principle, this means more frequent refunds. This also means that the responsibility is shared between the sending bank and the receiving bank. This shared responsibility incentivizes both parties to do more work in consumer protection and fraud detection. As the April deadline approaches, more details of the policy are beginning to emerge.
